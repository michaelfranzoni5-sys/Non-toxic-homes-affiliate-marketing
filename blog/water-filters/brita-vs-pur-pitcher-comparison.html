The Great Pitcher Debate — Brita or PUR?

If you've spent any time browsing water filter pitchers on Amazon, you've seen the two heavyweights: Brita and PUR. Together, they account for the overwhelming majority of pitcher sales in the United States. They sit side by side on store shelves, both hovering around the same price point, both promising clean, great-tasting water.

But here's the problem: they are not the same product, and the differences matter more than most people realize.

Brita is the household name — the brand you probably grew up with, owned by Clorox and backed by decades of marketing. PUR is owned by Helen of Troy and has historically positioned itself as the more health-focused option, with stronger NSF certifications for contaminant removal. Their filtration technologies differ, their certification claims differ, and their long-term costs differ significantly depending on which filter you choose.

NSF Certifications — What They Actually Certify

This is the single most important point of comparison, and where most buyers get confused. Both brands display the NSF logo on their packaging, but not all NSF certifications are equal.

Brita's Certification Tiers

Brita sells two types of replacement filters, and they have very different certification levels:

Filter NSF 42 NSF 53 NSF 401 Life Price (approx.)
Brita Standard ✅ Chlorine, taste, odor ❌ ❌ 40 gal / 2 mo $6–$8
Brita Longlast+ ✅ ✅ Lead, asbestos, mercury, cadmium ✅ Benzene, microplastics, pharmaceuticals 120 gal / 6 mo $12–$15

The critical takeaway: if you buy a Brita but use the Standard filter, you are not getting NSF 53 certification. That means no certified reduction of lead, asbestos, or other health-relevant contaminants. You need the Longlast+ filter for health-protective certification. This is a poorly understood distinction — many Brita users running Standard filters believe they're getting more protection than they actually are.

The Brita Longlast+ is also certified under NSF 401, which covers emerging compounds like pharmaceuticals (ibuprofen, estrone, etc.), BPA, and microplastics — the only pitcher filter on this list to hold that certification.

PUR's Certification Tiers

PUR keeps it simpler. All PUR Plus filters are certified under both NSF 42 and NSF 53:

Filter NSF 42 NSF 53 NSF 401 Life Price (approx.)
PUR Plus (all models) ✅ ✅ Lead, mercury, atrazine, asbestos ❌ 40 gal / 2 mo $8–$10
PUR Basic/Standard ✅ ❌ ❌ 40 gal / 2 mo $6–$8

PUR's Plus filter has been NSF 53 certified for years and is one of the best-known certifications for lead reduction in the pitcher category. PUR claims 99.6% reduction of lead, which is slightly higher than Brita's claimed 99.3%. PUR also certifies for mercury (99.5%), atrazine, and asbestos — none of which Brita's Standard filter covers.

The trade-off: PUR has no NSF 401 certification, so it doesn't formally claim reduction of pharmaceuticals or microplastics the way the Brita Longlast+ does.

Certification Verdict

Winner: Tie — but only if you compare the right models. The Brita Longlast+ wins on scope (NSF 42 + 53 + 401). The PUR Plus wins on lead and mercury reduction percentages. If you're using the cheapest filters from either brand, neither offers health-certified protection.

What This Means for You

If your primary concern is taste and odor (chlorine removal), both brands perform essentially identically. The difference becomes meaningful only when you need protection against specific contaminants:

  • Worried about lead? PUR Plus has the slight edge (99.6% vs 99.3%) with longer-standing NSF 53 certification.
  • Worried about mercury? PUR is significantly better (99.5% vs 96%).
  • Worried about pharmaceuticals or microplastics? Only the Brita Longlast+ covers these.
  • Worried about atrazine (well water / agricultural areas)? Only PUR covers this.

Neither filter removes PFAS, bacteria, viruses, or cysts. If those contaminants are a concern, look at under-sink reverse osmosis — read our countertop RO guide or our under-sink filter guide.

The Brita Longlast+ is dramatically cheaper over time than any other option. At approximately $0.08–$0.10 per gallon, it's half the cost of the PUR Plus ($0.16–$0.20/gal). The reason is the 6-month filter life — one Brita Longlast+ cartridge lasts as long as three PUR Plus cartridges.

Pitcher Design and Build Quality

Brita's Lineup

Brita offers the widest range of pitcher sizes and styles:

  • Brita UltraMax 27-cup Dispenser (~$35): The largest option on the market. Dishwasher safe, LED filter change indicator, space-saving design that fits in most refrigerator shelves.
  • Brita Stream 10-cup Pitcher (~$30): Slim design, finger-touch lid, filter indicator. Most popular model.
  • Brita Metro 6-cup Pitcher (~$20): Compact for small fridges. Good for singles or couples.
  • Brita Soho 10-cup Pitcher (~$25): Stainless steel accent, minimalist aesthetic.

Build quality is generally good. The plastic is BPA-free (all Brita pitchers are now BPA-free). The LED filter indicator on most models is a nice convenience. The lid design has improved over the years, but some users report that the UltraMax lid can pop off if the pitcher falls over.

PUR's Lineup

PUR's pitcher selection is smaller but well-designed:

  • PUR Ultimate 11-cup Dispenser (~$35): Large capacity, built-in faucet, sleek design. Fits in most refrigerators.
  • PUR Classic 11-cup Pitcher (~$25): Standard design with flip-lock lid and filter change light.
  • PUR 7-cup Pitcher (~$22): Compact option for small households.

PUR pitchers are known for their mineral clear technology — a design that maximizes water exposure to the carbon filter for better contaminant capture. The flip-lock lid on the Classic model is considered more secure than Brita's basic lids. The filter change indicator uses a battery-powered light, which some users find more visible than Brita's mechanical counter.

Design Verdict

Brita wins on variety — if you need a 27-cup dispenser, Brita is your only choice. PUR wins on build quality — the flip-lock lid and sturdier construction feel more premium. Both are BPA-free and dishwasher safe (top rack).

Taste and Flow Rate — Real-World Experience

Based on aggregated user reviews from Amazon, Reddit, and consumer testing:

Taste

Both filters remove chlorine taste effectively. In blind taste tests, most people cannot consistently distinguish between Brita- and PUR-filtered water. However, some common observations:

  • Brita users often report a slightly "cleaner" or "neutral" taste immediately after filtration.
  • PUR users sometimes report a faint "mineral" aftertaste, especially with a new filter. This dissipates after the first gallon or so.
  • Both are significantly better than tap water in areas with heavy chlorination.

Flow Rate

Flow rates are comparable, but Brita's larger filter surface area (especially on the Longlast+) tends to fill faster:

  • Brita (Longlast+): Fills a full pitcher in approximately 2–4 minutes
  • PUR (Plus): Fills in approximately 3–5 minutes
  • Brita (Standard): Fills in approximately 2–3 minutes

Winner: Brita (slight edge on flow rate).

Frequently Asked Questions

Do Brita or PUR filters remove fluoride?

No. Standard activated carbon filters do not remove fluoride. If fluoride reduction is a priority, you need reverse osmosis or a specialized bone char carbon filter. Our under-sink filter guide covers options that address fluoride.

How often should I actually replace the filter?

Both brands recommend every 2 months (Standard) or 6 months (Brita Longlast+). However, if your water is heavily chlorinated, you may need to change more frequently. A rule of thumb: if you notice a chlorine taste returning, replace the filter regardless of calendar timing.

Can I use a Brita filter in a PUR pitcher (or vice versa)?

No. The cartridges are physically different shapes and are not interchangeable. Brita uses a cylindrical cartridge; PUR uses a rectangular cartridge with a unique locking mechanism.

Which pitcher is best for well water?

Neither Brita nor PUR is designed specifically for well water. However, if you have a basic well with standard contaminant levels, PUR Plus is the better choice because its NSF 53 certification covers atrazine and other agricultural contaminants more commonly found in well water. For well water with bacteria concerns, you need UV filtration or a certified cyst-removal filter.

Are Brita and PUR filters BPA-free?

Yes. Both brands have transitioned to BPA-free plastic in all their pitcher and filter housings. Neither brand uses BPA in their current production.

Do these filters need to be refrigerated?

The filters do not require refrigeration. However, the filtered water tastes best when chilled, and a full refrigerator is more energy-efficient than an empty one. Most users keep their filled pitcher in the fridge.

Which one has better customer support?

Both brands offer standard 1-year warranties on pitchers. Brita has a larger customer service operation (Clorox-owned) and generally faster response times. PUR's customer service (Helen of Troy) is also responsive but has fewer support channels. Neither brand is known for outstanding or poor support — expect standard consumer-grade service.

The Bottom Line

After comparing NSF certifications, contaminant reduction data, cost-per-gallon over 5 years, pitcher design, and real-world user experiences, here's our final recommendation:

The best value pitcher is the Brita UltraMax + Longlast+ filter combination. You get the largest capacity, the lowest cost-per-gallon ($0.08–$0.10), NSF 42 + 53 + 401 certification, and the convenience of changing filters only twice per year. For a family of four, this saves approximately $70–$80 per year compared to using PUR Plus filters, while covering a broader range of emerging contaminants (pharmaceuticals, microplastics) that PUR doesn't address.

However, if you have confirmed lead or mercury in your water, choose PUR Plus. Its NSF 53 certification for these heavy metals is more established, its reduction percentages are higher, and every PUR Plus filter carries the same certification — you don't need to hunt for the right filter SKU.

For most households with standard municipal tap water, the Brita Longlast+ offers the best balance of cost, convenience, and certified protection. For homeowners with specific heavy metal concerns, PUR Plus is the safer choice.
