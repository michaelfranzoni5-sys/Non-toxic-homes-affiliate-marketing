What Are PFAS and Why Do They Matter?

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are a class of more than 9,000 synthetic chemicals used since the 1940s to make products resistant to heat, oil, stains, and water. You've encountered them in non-stick cookware, waterproof jackets, food packaging, firefighting foam, and countless industrial applications.

They earned the nickname "forever chemicals" because the carbon-fluorine bond in PFAS is among the strongest in organic chemistry — it does not break down in the environment or in the human body. PFAS accumulate in blood serum, and the half-life of common PFAS compounds ranges from 2–9 years in humans.

What the Science Says

  • The Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR) links PFAS exposure to increased cholesterol levels, reduced vaccine response in children, thyroid hormone disruption, pregnancy-induced hypertension, and kidney and testicular cancers.
  • A landmark 2023 USGS study detected PFAS in nearly 45% of US tap water samples — and that's testing for only 32 of the 9,000+ PFAS compounds. The real number is likely higher.
  • The EPA's 2024 National Primary Drinking Water Regulation sets enforceable maximum contaminant levels (MCLs) of 4 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS — a dramatic tightening from the previous 70 ppt health advisory.

The concern isn't acute poisoning — it's lifetime cumulative exposure. Even low levels of PFAS consumed daily over decades can reach biologically significant concentrations in your body.

Which Water Sources Are Most at Risk?

Not all tap water carries the same PFAS risk. Here's where contamination is most likely:

🏭 Municipal Water Supplies Near Industrial Sites

Communities near PFAS manufacturing plants, airports (firefighting foam training sites), military bases, landfills, and wastewater treatment plants face the highest risk. The Environmental Working Group (EWG) has mapped over 2,800+ PFAS contamination sites across the US.

🌾 Agricultural Areas (Well Water)

PFAS migrates through soil and can contaminate groundwater. Private wells in agricultural or industrial regions are not tested or regulated by the EPA — testing is the homeowner's responsibility. If you're on well water within a few miles of a known PFAS source, annual PFAS testing is strongly recommended.

🚰 Municipal Water Systems (Broad, Low-Level)

Even without a nearby industrial source, PFAS has been detected in major city water supplies — including New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, and Boston. The USGS study found that urban areas had higher detection rates than rural areas, but no region is immune. PFAS is now considered a ubiquitous environmental contaminant.

📦 Bottled Water (Not a Free Pass)

Bottled water is not automatically PFAS-free. A 2021 study tested 101 bottled water brands and found PFAS in 39% of them — including several major brands. Bottled water is also regulated by the FDA, not the EPA, and there are no federal PFAS limits for bottled water. Plus, it's dramatically more expensive and worse for the environment (see our complete water filter guide for cost comparisons).

NSF Certifications for PFAS — What They Actually Mean

NSF/ANSI standards are the gold standard for water filter certification in North America. When it comes to PFAS, three standards matter:

NSF 53 — Health Effects (PFAS Included)

Filters certified under NSF 53 are tested to reduce specific health-related contaminants including lead, mercury, VOCs, and — critically — PFOA and PFOS. Not all NSF 53 filters are tested for PFAS, so you must verify PFAS-specific claims. Look for filters that explicitly list PFOA/PFOS reduction in their NSF 53 testing data.

NSF 58 — Reverse Osmosis Systems

NSF 58 covers all under-sink reverse osmosis systems. RO is inherently effective at PFAS removal — the semipermeable membrane blocks molecules much smaller than PFAS compounds. WQA Gold Seal certification under NSF 58 (as with the iSpring RCC7) indicates the system has been independently tested.

NSF P473 — PFAS-Specific Testing

NSF P473 is a protocol specifically developed for testing PFAS reduction. This is the most targeted certification for PFAS. Filters carrying NSF P473 certification have been independently tested against PFOA, PFOS, and other PFAS compounds. Currently, only select carbon block and RO systems hold this certification.

What About Boiling? Distillation? Bottled Water?

Three common questions answered directly:

❓ Does boiling water remove PFAS?

No. Boiling concentrates PFAS. As water evaporates, PFAS molecules remain and become more concentrated in the remaining water. Never boil PFAS-contaminated water thinking it will help.

❓ Does distillation remove PFAS?

Yes — mostly. Distillation boils water and captures the steam, leaving most contaminants behind. However, some volatile PFAS compounds can vaporize and re-condense. Distillation is effective but slow and energy-intensive. A quality RO or carbon block filter is more practical for daily use.

❓ Is bottled water PFAS-free?

Not guaranteed. A 2021 study found PFAS in 39% of tested bottled water brands. Bottled water is regulated by the FDA, which has not set PFAS limits. You're paying more for uncertain protection. A home filter is cheaper, more reliable, and better for the environment.

How to Test Your Water for PFAS

Before buying a filter, confirm whether PFAS is actually in your water. Here's how:

  1. Check your Consumer Confidence Report (CCR) — Every US municipal water utility sends one annually. Look for PFAS testing results. Some utilities test voluntarily; others are now required to under new EPA rules.
  2. Use the EWG Tap Water Database — Enter your zip code to see known contaminants in your area. The EWG cross-references utility reports with independent testing.
  3. Order a lab test — For definitive results, order a PFAS-specific water test from a certified lab like Tap Score or SimpleLab. These cost ~$200–400 and test for 14–40+ PFAS compounds.
  4. If you're on well water — You must arrange testing yourself. No utility tests for you. Contact your county health department or a certified water testing lab.

Frequently Asked Questions

❓ Can I trust my municipal water report for PFAS?

Partially. Many utilities now test for PFAS, but most test for only a handful of the 9,000+ PFAS compounds. The EPA's 2024 rule mandates testing for six PFAS compounds — PFOA, PFOS, PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA (GenX), and PFBS. Your water could contain other unmonitored PFAS variants.

❓ What's the difference between PFOA, PFOS, GenX, and other PFAS?

PFOA and PFOS are the two most studied and regulated PFAS compounds — they were phased out of US production in the 2000s but persist in the environment. GenX and PFBS are newer "replacement" PFAS chemicals introduced after PFOA/PFOS were phased out. Many are also toxic. The best filters target all PFAS, not just the legacy ones.

❓ Are whole-house PFAS filters worth it?

For most households, no. PFAS exposure is primarily through drinking water, not showering or bathing. A point-of-use filter (under-sink or countertop) at your kitchen sink covers 95%+ of your PFAS ingestion risk. Whole-house PFAS systems (typically granular activated carbon tanks) cost $1,000–$4,000+ and require significant maintenance.

❓ How often should I replace a PFAS filter?

Follow the manufacturer's recommendation, but note that PFAS may exhaust filter capacity faster than chlorine or sediment. The Aquasana Claryum is rated for 800 gallons (1 year). If you have high PFAS levels, consider replacing carbon block filters at 75% of their rated lifespan. RO membranes last 2–3 years regardless.

❓ Does a PFAS filter also remove other contaminants?

Yes — and this is one of the reasons to invest in a certified system. The Aquasana Claryum also removes lead, mercury, VOCs, pharmaceuticals, pesticides, and microplastics. The iSpring RCC7 removes virtually everything. You're not buying a single-contaminant solution.
