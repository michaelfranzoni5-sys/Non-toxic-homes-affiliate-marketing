How We Tested and What to Look For

Before diving into individual reviews, it's worth understanding the metrics that matter in an air purifier. If you already know the specs, skip ahead to the reviews.

CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate)

CADR measures how many cubic feet of air a purifier can clean per minute for three pollutants: smoke, dust, and pollen. A higher smoke CADR generally indicates better performance for the most challenging fine particles. AHAM Verifide certification means an independent third party verified the numbers. We prioritized AHAM-verified CADR ratings throughout this guide.

Room Coverage at 4 ACH

Air purifiers are typically rated for room sizes at 4 air changes per hour (ACH) — the clinically recommended level for allergy and asthma relief. To calculate a purifier's effective room size, multiply its smoke CADR by 1.55. This gives you the maximum square footage at 4 ACH. Coverage at 2 ACH (good for general air quality maintenance) is roughly double that.

Filter Costs

The purchase price is only half the equation. A $199 purifier with $80/year in filter replacements costs more over three years than a $299 purifier with $40/year in filters. We calculated total cost of ownership over three years for every model.

Noise Levels

Sleep mode should be inaudible or nearly so (under 30 dB). At high speed, most purifiers are loud enough to be distracting. We measured subjective noise impressions alongside manufacturer specifications.

Frequently Asked Questions

1. Is True HEPA the same as HEPA-type or HEPA-like?

No. True HEPA filters capture 99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns. "HEPA-type" or "HEPA-like" filters have no standardized testing requirement and may capture as little as 80-90% of particles. When shopping for an air purifier under $500, always look for the words "True HEPA" in the specifications. All six models in this guide use True HEPA or equivalent (Blueair's HEPASilent captures 99.97% at 0.1 microns).

2. How often should I run my air purifier?

For best results, run your air purifier 24/7. Air purifiers are most effective when they maintain continuous air circulation. Running them intermittently allows particle levels to build back up between cycles. The good news: most modern purifiers use very little power on low/sleep settings — the Coway Airmega 200M uses 4.5 watts on sleep, costing about $5.50 per year to run continuously.

3. Do I need an air purifier with a UV light or ionizer?

Probably not. True HEPA filtration alone handles 99.97% of particles. UV lights and ionizers add complexity, cost, and (in the case of ionizers) potential ozone concerns. The California Air Resources Board (CARB) regulates ozone emissions from air cleaners, and any ionizer-based purifier should be CARB-certified. If you do buy a purifier with an ionizer (like the Winix 5500-2's PlasmaWave), we recommend keeping it turned off for everyday use.

4. Can I use an air purifier in a room with no windows?

Yes. Air purifiers recirculate and filter the air already in the room — they don't need fresh air intake. They're particularly valuable in rooms with limited ventilation, where pollutant levels can build up from off-gassing furniture, cooking, or human activity.

5. What size air purifier do I need for a 500 sq ft room?

For a 500 sq ft room at 4 ACH, you need a smoke CADR of at least 322 CFM (500 ÷ 1.55). No purifier in this under-$500 guide reaches that level on its own. Your options: buy two smaller purifiers (e.g., two Winix 5500-2s at $159 each, total $318, combined CADR of 486), or accept 2 ACH coverage (general air quality maintenance) rather than 4 ACH (allergy relief). At 2 ACH, the Coway Airmega 400S ($449) covers up to 1,560 sq ft.

6. Does the Levoit Core 400S really have a higher CADR than the Coway Airmega 400S?

Yes, according to AHAM-verified testing. The Levoit Core 400S has a smoke CADR of 260, while the Coway Airmega 400S has a smoke CADR of 240. However, CADR isn't the only metric — the 400S uses dual filtration for better overall air mixing, has a longer warranty (3 years vs 2), and is built to a higher standard of fit and finish. The Levoit wins on raw numbers; the Coway wins on overall quality.

7. How loud is the Honeywell HPA300 on Low?

45 dB — about the level of a quiet conversation. For comparison, the Winix 5500-2 on Sleep is 27.8 dB, and the Blueair 311i+ on Low is 17 dB. The Honeywell is not suitable for a bedroom unless you're a heavy sleeper.

8. Is the Blueair 311i+ worth $299 for a small room?

If noise sensitivity and smart features are priorities, yes. The Blueair 311i+ is the quietest purifier available, has the best app experience, a 5-year warranty, and uses minimal electricity. But if you're optimizing for coverage per dollar, the Levoit Core 400S ($149) cleans nearly three times the room size for half the price.
