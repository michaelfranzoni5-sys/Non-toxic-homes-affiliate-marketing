Understanding Indoor Allergens

Before choosing a purifier, it helps to understand what you're filtering — because not all particles are created equal, and not all filters catch everything.

Pollen (10–100 μm)

Seasonal pollen from trees, grasses, and weeds is large enough to be caught by almost any filter. But because pollen is buoyant and recirculates easily, even a high-end purifier needs to cycle the room's air 4+ times per hour to keep levels low during peak seasons.

Dust Mite Debris (1–20 μm)

You're not actually allergic to dust mites — you're allergic to their fecal pellets and decomposing body fragments. These particles are tiny, easily resuspended by footsteps, and a primary trigger for perennial allergic rhinitis.

Pet Dander (0.5–5 μm)

Cat and dog dander particles are smaller than pollen and can remain airborne for hours. They also adhere to upholstery and bedding, so a purifier alone won't solve pet allergies — but it's an essential complement to regular vacuuming and washing.

Mold Spores (1–30 μm)

Mold spores are opportunistic. They enter through open doors and windows or thrive in damp basements and bathrooms. Once airborne, they circulate through the HVAC system. A purifier with a true HEPA filter and an activated carbon layer can help capture spores and adsorb the musty VOCs they release.

What to Look For in an Air Purifier

True HEPA vs. HEPA-Type

True HEPA filters meet the strict standard of capturing 99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns — the "most penetrating particle size" (MPPS). HEPA-type, "HEPA-like," or "HEPA-style" filters have no standardized performance requirement. They may capture 90–95% of larger particles but will let smaller allergens slip through.

Our advice: For allergies, buy True HEPA. The price difference is usually $20–40, and the performance gap for sub-micron allergens like pet dander and dust mite debris is significant.

CADR Ratings (Clean Air Delivery Rate)

CADR measures how quickly a purifier removes smoke (0.1–1 μm), dust (0.5–3 μm), and pollen (5–11 μm) from a room. Ratings are in cubic feet per minute (CFM). The Association of Home Appliance Manufacturers (AHAM) certifies these numbers.

Rule of thumb: Choose a purifier whose CADR for your target allergen is at least ⅔ of the room's square footage. For a 300 sq. ft. room, look for a smoke CADR of 200+.

Room Size Matching (ACH)

Most manufacturers quote a "maximum room size" based on 1 air change per hour (ACH). Allergists recommend 4 ACH for significant symptom relief. That means the purifier needs to handle 4× the room volume per hour.

Quick math: If a purifier claims a 500 sq. ft. max room size at 1 ACH, it's realistically suited for about 125 sq. ft. at 4 ACH.

Filter Replacement Costs

This is the hidden expense that surprises most buyers. A $150 purifier with $80 filters every 6 months costs $310/year to operate in year two. Check: - How often do filters need replacing? (Manufacturer claims vs. real-world) - Are third-party replacements available, or is it proprietary? - Does the unit have a washable pre-filter that extends HEPA life?

Noise Levels

Sleep-friendly operation matters. Look for models with a "sleep" or "night" mode that runs below 30 dB — quieter than a whisper. The best units are virtually inaudible at low speed.

FAQ

Can an air purifier cure my allergies?

No. Air purifiers reduce airborne allergen concentrations, which can significantly relieve symptoms — but they don't eliminate the source. Combine a purifier with regular cleaning, dust-mite-proof bedding, and HEPA vacuuming for best results.

How often should I replace the filter?

Manufacturers typically recommend every 6–12 months. Real-world lifespan depends on air quality: if you smoke, have pets, or live in a dusty area, plan to replace every 6 months. Watch for the "replace filter" indicator, but also trust your nose — if the air smells stale, the carbon layer is exhausted.

Should I get a purifier with an ionizer?

Ionizers can cause particles to stick to surfaces rather than being captured in the filter, and some produce trace amounts of ozone. We recommend purifiers that use mechanical (HEPA) filtration as the primary mechanism. If you buy one with an ionizer, make sure it can be turned off (like the Winix 5500-2).

Do I need a purifier in every room?

No. Focus on the rooms where you spend the most time: the bedroom (8+ hours/night) and the main living area. If you have a single-story home, one high-CADR unit (like the Coway 400S or Honeywell HPA300) in a central hallway can often suffice.

What about portable vs. whole-house systems?

Portable room purifiers are more effective per dollar than whole-house HVAC add-ons. A $450 Coway will outperform a $2,000 HVAC system upgrade in a single room. Whole-house systems make sense for new construction or major renovations.

Do air purifiers help with wildfire smoke?

Yes. For wildfire smoke, prioritize high smoke CADR (200+) and a purifier with a good activated carbon layer for odor removal. The IQAir HealthPro 250 and Coway Airmega 400S both excel in these conditions.
